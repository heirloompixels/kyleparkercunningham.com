All prints are intaglio drypoint prints, a technique that originated in the fifteenth century CE. Each print is hand pulled on a two ton, one hundred year old etching press.

The plates are each scratched with a scribe point, inked, and then wiped clean leaving ink deposited in the scratches. The plate, with a piece of wet paper set upon it, is then rolled through the press where an extraordinary amount of pressure pushes the wet paper into the incised lines, transferring the ink and creating the print. The prints are then dried and pressed flat.

Intaglio printmaking is monochromatic; color is added afterward by hand and brush with watercolor or gouache, making each print an individually unique piece of art. Prints are numbered sequentially as printed, one at a time, over the years — the hand-scratched plates limit each edition to about 30 prints before the plate is deformed beyond use.

This is the ongoing **Ephemera** series: an examination of the glitches experienced in day to day reality.
